95% of us aren't getting enough fiber.

Fiber is the indigestible part of plant foods that moves through your digestive system, feeding beneficial gut bacteria, regulating bowel habits, and improving metabolic health. This guide explains how to increase your fiber intake in a practical, sustainable way.

Fiber can help with

Constipation Diarrhea & loose stools IBS Blood sugar control High cholesterol Appetite & weight

Dietary fiber

Start with food.

Increasing fiber in your diet is one of the most consistently beneficial changes you can make for your health. Higher dietary fiber intake is associated with better bowel regularity, lower cholesterol, improved blood sugar control, and reduced risk of colorectal cancer and cardiovascular disease. The targets are 25g per day for women and 38g per day for men.

Most people fall well short of these goals. The good news is that small, consistent swaps — rather than a complete diet overhaul — are often enough to make a meaningful difference.

Practical ways to increase dietary fiber ▼

Breakfast

  • Swap white bread or bagels for whole grain toast (2–3g fiber per slice)
  • Choose oatmeal over refined cereals — rolled or steel-cut oats provide 4g per serving
  • Add ground flaxseed or chia seeds to yogurt, oatmeal, or a smoothie (2–5g per tablespoon)
  • Look for cereals with at least 5g of fiber per serving; avoid those where sugar is a top ingredient
  • Add berries — raspberries and blackberries are among the highest-fiber fruits (4–8g per cup)

Lunch

  • Legumes are the highest-fiber lunch option available — a cup of cooked lentils, chickpeas, black beans, kidney beans, or pinto beans provides 12–15g of fiber on its own
  • Lentil soup, chickpea salads, bean tacos, rice and beans, or a grain bowl with legumes are all practical ways to build a high-fiber lunch around these ingredients
  • Hummus with whole wheat pita or raw vegetables is an easy 4–8g addition
  • Edamame as a side dish provides 8g per cup
  • Replace white rice with brown rice when possible — or mix half and half if the swap feels too abrupt

Dinner

  • Fill half your plate with vegetables — broccoli, bok choy, Brussels sprouts, spinach, snow peas, artichokes, and cactus (nopales) are all high-fiber options that fit naturally into many cooking styles
  • Beans and lentils belong at dinner too — chickpea or lentil stews, black bean soups, and dishes where legumes are cooked into a sauce or curry easily reach 10–15g of fiber per meal
  • Bulgur, brown rice, or whole wheat bread as a base adds 3–5g compared to refined grain alternatives
  • Tofu and vegetable stir-fries, grain and vegetable soups, or stuffed vegetables are all naturally fiber-dense meals
  • Roasted vegetables with the skin on retain more fiber than peeled or boiled ones

Snacks

  • An apple or pear with the skin on provides 4–5g of fiber
  • A small handful of almonds or pistachios adds 3g of fiber
  • Hummus with raw vegetables (carrots, celery, bell pepper) is a reliable 4–6g snack
  • Popcorn (air-popped) provides about 3.5g per 3-cup serving
  • Avoid fiber bars as a primary source — many have very little actual fiber and are high in added sugar

In liquids

  • Add a tablespoon of ground flaxseed to a morning smoothie — it blends in without changing the flavor
  • Blend spinach or kale into fruit smoothies for 1–2g of fiber per handful with minimal taste impact
  • Psyllium husk powder can be stirred into water, juice, or a smoothie as a supplement (see below)
  • Avoid relying on fiber-fortified drinks or juices as your primary source — they often contain added sugar and low-quality fiber

Supplemental fiber

Not all fiber supplements work the same way.

When diet alone isn't enough to close the gap, supplements can help. But what determines whether a fiber supplement actually works is whether it forms a gel when it meets water in your gut.

Gel-forming fibers

  • Psyllium husk
  • Beta-glucan (oats, barley)
  • Raw guar gum

Non-gel-forming fibers

  • Inulin
  • Wheat dextrin (Benefiber)
  • FOS (fructooligosaccharides)
  • Methylcellulose (Citrucel)

The most extensively studied gel-forming fiber in human clinical trials is psyllium husk — the active ingredient in Metamucil and many store-brand fiber supplements.

How it works

What psyllium actually does in your body.

Psyllium is over 80% fiber by weight. When it reaches your digestive system, it absorbs water and forms a thick, slippery gel. That gel does four distinct things:

🪣

Softens and bulks stool (for constipation)

🧽

Absorbs excess water and slows transit (for diarrhea)

📉

Slows sugar absorption after meals

🔗

Removes cholesterol-related bile acids

What to expect

The first two weeks can feel rough. That's normal.

The most common reason people stop taking fiber is bloating and gas in the first week or two. Understanding why this happens makes it much easier to push through.

🦠

Why you get gas and bloating

Gut bacteria need 1–2 weeks to adapt to psyllium. Until then, fermentation runs higher than normal, producing extra gas.

💧

Why you feel full or pressured

Psyllium shifts water distribution in your colon before it has adapted, creating temporary fullness or pressure. This also resolves within 1–2 weeks.

🔁

If you stop and restart, the clock resets

Stopping resets your gut bacteria's adaptation. Restarting produces the same side effects again — so it's better to lower the dose and ramp more slowly than to stop entirely.

How to take it

Start low. Increase slowly. Give it four weeks.

The most common reason psyllium doesn't work is not that it's the wrong product — it's that patients either start too high and stop due to side effects, or start too low and never reach an effective dose. Follow this ramp schedule and give it the full assessment window before deciding whether it's working.

1Week

3–4 g once daily

Powder: 1 teaspoon in a full glass of water, once daily. Capsules: 5 capsules with 1.5 glasses of water, once daily.

Drink immediately — don't let it sit and gel.

2Week

6–8 g twice daily

Powder: 1 teaspoon, twice daily. Capsules: 5 capsules, twice daily.

If you experienced gas or bloating in week one, stay at once daily for another week before increasing.

3–4Weeks

9–12 g three times daily

Powder: 1 teaspoon, three times daily. Capsules: 5 capsules, three times daily. Taking a dose before each meal works well for most people.

4+Weeks

Target dose: 10–15 g daily

This is the therapeutic range where most clinical studies show benefit. Wait at least 4 full weeks at this dose before assessing whether it's working.

Water is not optional. Psyllium begins gelling the moment it contacts liquid. Take powder with a minimum of 8 oz (one full glass) and drink it immediately. Capsules require even more — 10–12 oz per dose.

Safety & interactions

When to be careful.

Psyllium is safe for long-term daily use for most people. There are a few important situations where you should either adjust timing or consult your doctor first.

Medication timing

Because psyllium gel can physically absorb compounds in the gut — the same mechanism it uses to trap bile acids — it can reduce absorption of certain medications if taken at the same time. Separate psyllium from these medications by the indicated window:

Levothyroxine (thyroid)
Separate by at least 4 hours. Taking together may reduce thyroid hormone absorption.
Warfarin
Separate by 2 hours. Have your INR checked when starting psyllium.
Digoxin
Separate by 2 hours.
Lithium
Separate by 2 hours. Monitor lithium levels at initiation.
Insulin / sulfonylureas
Monitor blood glucose closely when starting psyllium, as improved glycemic control may increase hypoglycemia risk in patients already on these medications.

Medical Disclaimer

This page is intended for general patient education only. It does not constitute medical advice, and it does not replace the guidance of your physician, gastroenterologist, or other qualified healthcare provider. Individual responses to fiber supplementation vary. If you have specific medical conditions — including swallowing difficulties, inflammatory bowel disease, known food allergies, or complex medication regimens — consult your provider before starting psyllium.
